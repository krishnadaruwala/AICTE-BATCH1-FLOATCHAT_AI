## Abstract

Long-term analysis of salinity can be useful to describe the main mechanisms that operate at the surface of the ocean. Average sea surface salinity (SSS) contour plots for the Indian Ocean are produced based on the NODC_WOA94 data provided by the NOAA/OAR/ESRL PSL. Salinity, together with the independent variables wind, ndff (net-down-fresh-water-flow), and Ekman pumping, are included in a multiple regression analysis to define the relative importance of each of these variables in the physical processes at the surface of the Indian Ocean. The ndff data set is based on COADS (Comprehensive Ocean-Atmosphere Data Set). The wind data is obtained from the Florida State University (FSU).

The harmonic terms are considered stationary and expressed by Fourier series as a cosine function in which the first and second harmonic terms are multiplied by the maximum amplitude of the variable and added to the mean annual parameter. The salinity contours tend to be zonally orientated away from the coast, while a meridional influence is observed close to the boundaries. A typical zonal pattern of salinity distribution is observed only south of 10°S. Maximum annual amplitude values are observed in the north of the Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal.

The variability of the annual components is consistent with the distribution of the net-down-freshwater-flow (ndff) contours and wind direction. During the SW Monsoon, the ndff becomes gradually positive towards the east, in the direction of the west coast of India, resulting in a peak of maximum salinity in August and decreasing afterwards. During the NE Monsoon, the ndff is negative elsewhere in the Arabian Sea. The annual term plays a dominant role in determining the maximum and minimum salinity observed during August and January, while the semi-annual component provides minor adjustment. The annual component shows the influence of the monsoons through the year, with high salinity during the NE Monsoon and a secondary peak during the SW Monsoon. Although harmonic analysis can be applied to the study of salinity variability, to identify and quantify the variables related to these areas of large annual and semiannual variability, a multiple regression analysis needs to be applied.

## 1. Introduction

The northern Indian Ocean is usually subdivided into two different subregions according to differences in surface water properties: the Arabian Sea, having high-salinity water, and the Bay of Bengal, having low-salinity water. Salinity increases from west to east and from the equator towards the north. The eastern part of the Indian Ocean is mostly affected by the rainfall region around Sumatra, which contributes to the low sea surface salinity observed in the Bay of Bengal.

In general terms, surface circulation in the Indian Ocean follows the wind pattern. The Indian Ocean can be organized into three general circulation systems: the seasonal monsoon gyre, the subtropical anticyclonic gyre of the Southern Hemisphere, and the Antarctic waters with the circumpolar current. It is, in fact, the seasonal reversal of the monsoonal gyre that gives the Indian Ocean its principal and unique characteristics.

During the NE Monsoon, a thermohaline circulation with northward transport against the wind direction occurs along the west coast of India. This northward current is generated by differences in pressure gradient from north to south caused by differences in temperature and salinity during winter. A remarkable semi-annual event in the study area is the Equatorial Jet — a strong eastward jet flow that occurs during the transition between the monsoons. The Equatorial Jet, flowing between 2°N and 2°S, results in the deepening of the thermocline along the Sumatran coast and uplift of the thermocline along the African coast. It represents a semi-annual reversal of flow triggered mainly by zonal wind, although equatorial Kelvin waves at greater depths also play a role. The situation reverses when wind speed decreases and a westward flow, together with the South Equatorial Current, replaces the water off Africa.

The salinity is below or close to 34 for most of the year in the eastern part of the Indian Ocean, along 10°S. This low salinity is due to the proximity of the AAMW (Australasian Mediterranean Water), which enters the Indian Ocean between the Indonesian islands and north of Australia. Another interesting aspect of Indian Ocean circulation is the weak eastern boundary current along the Australian coast compared to the strong western boundary current along the African coast (Agulhas Current).

This work describes the salinity distribution in the surface layer of the tropical Indian Ocean (25°N to 30°S and 30°E to 120°E), based on analysis of semi-annual and annual amplitudes of temperature, and a multiple regression analysis between temperature, Ekman pumping, components of pseudo-stress wind, wind magnitude, and net-down-freshwater-flux.

## 2. Material and Methods

A set of 5° grid average sea surface temperature (SSTS) contour plots for the Indian Ocean was produced based on the NODC_WOA94 data provided by the NOAA/OAR/ESRL PSL. Salinity, together with wind, ndff (net-down-fresh-water-flow), and Ekman pumping, was included in a multiple regression analysis to determine the relative importance of each variable in the physical processes at the surface of the Indian Ocean.

The ndff data set is based on COADS (Comprehensive Ocean-Atmosphere Data Set). It was originally organized in a 2×2 grid and averaged into a 5×5 grid for this research. The ndff represents the balance between precipitation and evaporation (P–E), with units in mm.

The wind data is obtained from the Florida State University (FSU) Wind Stress Data Set for the tropical Indian Ocean, on a 1×1 grid, and is averaged into a 5×5 grid. The FSU data set is based on COADS and NCDC (National Climatic Data Centre). Wind stress is presented as pseudo-stress wind, which is the wind component multiplied by the wind magnitude.

For salinity, the twelve monthly mean values are subjected to a Harmonic Analysis to determine two harmonic components: one with a period of one year, and the other with a period of six months. This process examines how the two components contribute and interact to produce the more complex cycle of the observed and modeled data.

The harmonic terms are considered stationary, as the averages and corresponding autocorrelations are not expected to vary significantly over a long period. The stationary time series of salinity can be expressed symbolically by a Fourier series as a cosine function, in which the first and second harmonic terms are multiplied by the maximum amplitude of the variable and added to the mean annual parameter. The modeled data is expressed as:

```
O(t) = Ō + A × cos(ωa × t − a) + B × cos(ωs × t − b)
```

where:
- **O(t)** = observed value as a function of time, where *t* is time in days since the start of an arbitrary year
- **Ō** = mean value over the period analyzed
- **A** = amplitude of the annual harmonic component
- **B** = amplitude of the semi-annual harmonic component
- **a** = phase-lag of the annual component
- **b** = phase-lag of the semi-annual component
- **ωa** = angular frequency (2π / 365.25), the increment in phase per unit time of the annual harmonic component
- **ωs** = angular frequency (2π / 182.62), the equivalent for the semi-annual component
- **(ωa × t − a)** = phase of the annual harmonic
- **(ωs × t − b)** = phase of the semi-annual harmonic

Values of O, A, B, a, and b are determined by analysis. O represents the average annual salinity and is a positive constant. The amplitude (A or B) is also a positive constant representing the amplitude of the motion — i.e., its maximum departure from the mean value as the term oscillates within a range of + or −.

Phase-lag is expressed in degrees, incrementing at 30° per month for the first harmonic and 60° per month for the second. Where phase is zero, a maximum occurs at the beginning of January. It expresses the average time when the maximum of SSS occurs within the annual cycle — for example, a phase of 120° corresponds to the first of April, 150° to the first of May, and 270° to the first of October.

The sum of squared residuals is defined as:

```
SS_res = Σ(Y − Y')²
```

representing the sum of squared differences between observed (Y) and predicted (Y') values, indicating small inadequacies of the fit.

## 3. Results

The 5° grid average sea surface salinity (SSS) contour plots during the SW Monsoon (July, August, and September) show that salinity contours tend to be zonally orientated away from the coast, with a meridional influence observed close to the boundaries. A typical zonal pattern of salinity distribution is observed only south of 10°S; elsewhere, the salinity contours follow a more meridional pattern.

The annual variation of salinity, expressed through the amplitude of the annual (first) and semi-annual (second) harmonics, shows annual amplitude values higher than 0.20 in the north of the Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal, along the southwest coast of India in the Arabian Sea, and towards the north along the western and eastern coastlines of the Bay of Bengal. For most of the central Indian Ocean, the annual amplitude of salinity is close to or lower than 0.20.

At 72.5°E and 7.5°N, along the southwest coast of India (where large salinity amplitudes are found), the annual component exhibits a maximum in August and a minimum in February. The semi-annual component is smaller in magnitude than the annual component, with two maxima in April and October and two minima in July and January. Phase-lags are only shown for regions where amplitudes exceed 0.20 (northern Indian Ocean). Residual errors between observed and harmonic salinity are below 0.09 for most of the data, with large residuals observed from September to November (maximum of 0.44 in October). The small magnitude of residual errors indicates that salinity variability through the year can be reasonably explained by just the two harmonic components.

The distribution of the salinity phase-lag for the annual harmonic component shows that in the Arabian Sea, maximum annual amplitude of salinity is found at the end of August, along the southwest coast of India. The central Bay of Bengal exhibits maximum annual amplitude at late August. In the southern Indian Ocean, the maximum of the annual component occurs at the end of September at 10°S, and in late July when close to 30°S.

At 82.5°E and 17.5°N (western Bay of Bengal), two salinity maxima are observed during May and December, and two minima during February and August. The amplitudes for the annual and semi-annual harmonic terms are similar in magnitude. The annual component has a maximum in March (peak of the NE Monsoon) and a minimum in August (peak of the SW Monsoon), suggesting a connection with the monsoons. For the semi-annual harmonic term, two maxima occur during May/June and October/November, and two minima during February/March and July/August — the minima associated with the transitions between monsoons, and the maxima with the peaks of the monsoons.

The annual and semi-annual harmonics from January to April are in opposition, producing a minor low trough of salinity during February. The two components act in conjunction during August and September, resulting in a pronounced minimum during that period.

## 4. Discussion

The highest salinity, found in the north of the Arabian Sea and increasing from the equator southward, while the lowest salinity values are observed in the Bay of Bengal, agrees with the influence of ndff in the eastern equatorial zone and the Bay of Bengal. For example, a maximum positive ndff of 160 mm/yr is observed at the equator at about 95°E and 5°S, coinciding with low salinity of about 34.10. Conversely, two evaporative basins in the western Indian Ocean south of the equator coincide with salinity values above 35.00.

The low-salinity tongue (salinities below 35.00) observed along 10°S results from the westward transport of low-salinity waters from the Australasian Mediterranean Sea. To the north of the Bay of Bengal, the annual amplitude can be close to 2.00, influenced by river runoff from the Ganga and Brahmaputra rivers and by advection of low-salinity water from the Bay of Bengal. For most of the central Indian Ocean, the annual amplitude of salinity remains close to or lower than 0.20 — a pattern comparable to the North Sea, where higher amplitudes occur near the inflow of the Rhine and Elbe rivers and lower amplitudes occur in the open sea.

In the northern Indian Ocean, the latest occurrence of the annual maximum (end of August) is found in the southeast Arabian Sea and the Bay of Bengal. In the central Arabian Sea, the annual component peaks from late April to early July. This variability is consistent with the distribution of ndff contours: during the SW Monsoon, ndff becomes gradually positive towards the east (toward the west coast of India), producing a peak of maximum salinity in August that decreases afterward; during the NE Monsoon, ndff is negative elsewhere in the Arabian Sea.

The strong meridional gradient observed in the central Bay of Bengal (maximum of the annual component from early May to early September) can be related to the lowest salinity occurring in August, which coincides with maximum ndff. In the central Bay of Bengal, the maximum of the annual component occurring in early September and early May to the east of the region may also result from the ndff distribution, which is larger along the eastern boundaries of the Bay of Bengal.

The maximum annual component (August) and minimum (February) show a connection with summer and winter. During August (peak of the SW Monsoon), the maximum of the annual component is associated with maximum salinity — counterintuitive given this is the wet season with high precipitation in India, which would typically suggest low salinity. This is explained by the influence of the East Arabian Current, which advects high-salinity water from west to east in the Arabian Sea. During February (peak of the NE Monsoon), circulation reverses and a westward flow dominates the Arabian Sea. The low salinity associated with the annual minimum may result from the influence of the early East Indian Winter Jet (EIWJ), which — fully established by November — supplies fresh, low-density water from the Bay of Bengal into the Arabian Sea.

The two minima of the semi-annual component (July and January) relate to the onset of the SW and NE Monsoons, while the two maxima occur later, closer to the transition months between monsoons. The annual term plays a dominant role in determining the maximum and minimum salinity observed during August and January, while the semi-annual component provides only minor adjustment.

From mid-February to mid-April, the first maximum of the annual component appears mainly wind-driven: the anticyclonic flow (EIC) in the Bay of Bengal brings higher-salinity water from the equator and along the eastern Indian coast. The second maximum, from mid-July to mid-September, relates to the East Arabian Current bringing high-salinity water from the Arabian Sea along the western Indian coast — visible as an increase in salinity from August to November.

The two salinity highs are associated with the two maxima of the semi-annual components, and the two lows with the two minima, related to the NE and SW Monsoon peaks. During the NE Monsoon, this acts in opposition to the annual cycle and may relate to a local weak anticyclonic flow. During the SW Monsoon, low salinity in July and August may relate to increased precipitation, subsequently suppressed by high-salinity water influence from the Arabian Sea.

The two maxima occur during monsoon transitions when winds are weaker, but the area is likely influenced by higher-salinity water advected by the East Arabian Current. In November, the anticyclonic flow is replaced by the East Indian Winter Jet, bringing low-salinity water from the equator into the Bay of Bengal. From May, large precipitation and river runoff decrease sea-surface salinity. Care must be taken interpreting SW Monsoon salinity variability, as large residual errors are observed during this period.

It is suggested that minimum and maximum salinities may result from different processes such as precipitation and wind direction. For example, the zonal wind stress component is westward during January and February and eastward during August. The winter minima could reflect freshwater advected from the Indonesian archipelago, likely associated with the direction of wind stress components (eastward and southward) as negative ndff is observed during this season — an indication that advection processes are important in the salinity decrease during this period.

The summer minimum could partly result from high precipitation along the eastern boundaries of the Bay of Bengal during this season. During the SW Monsoon, winds carry moist air from the central Arabian Sea towards India, where it precipitates; high precipitation combined with river runoff results in strong salinity gradients in the Bay of Bengal, especially along the coast where freshwater input is larger. The maxima observed during May and November, at the monsoon transitions, can be associated with decreased wind-induced evaporation combined with advection processes.

The importance of the Wy and Wx variables in the regression equation can be associated with advection processes in the western Arabian Sea region. During summer (SW Monsoon), the East Arabian Current dominates, advecting high salinity from west to east; this reverses during winter (NE Monsoon), advecting low-salinity waters westward. The increase in salinity from February to June in the eastern Arabian Sea follows the increase in Wx and Wmg. Ekman pumping shows a dramatic increase in downwelling from May to July, then an abrupt increase from July to November. The minimum of Ekman pumping from June to August coincides with the maximum of the pseudo-stress wind components and wind magnitude.

---

## 5. Conclusions

Large annual and semiannual amplitudes of salinity were identified in the Bay of Bengal and along the western Indian coast. Salinity could only be explained by the independent variables in a few locations, likely due to advection processes — for example, the reversal of zonal circulation in the Arabian Sea between monsoons, which transports high-salinity water eastward during the SW Monsoon and westward during the NE Monsoon, producing a strong meridional salinity gradient in the central Arabian Sea.

In the north of the Bay of Bengal, river runoff also appears to play a major role in sea surface salinity variability, especially in coastal areas. The difficulty in predicting salinity along the western Arabian Sea can be explained by advection of low-salinity water masses from the Southern Hemisphere during the SW Monsoon — an influence not considered in the correlation analysis. Coastal upwelling is also not treated as an independent variable in the multiple regression analysis; instead, the approach used relates wind components to coastline orientation. This approach is not very satisfactory along the western Arabian Sea, where coastal upwelling is important.

Although salinity could not be fully explained by the variables tested, the analysis of individual variables (ndff and pseudo-stress wind components), combined with harmonic analysis results, provides a reasonable picture of the processes related to salinity variability. Salinity tends to exhibit a uniform pattern of distribution over the ocean's surface, except for the Bay of Bengal region and the eastern equatorial region.
